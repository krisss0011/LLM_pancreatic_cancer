Optimal drug application on stochastic cancer growth: an
approach through path integral control
Noelymar Farinacci* ‚Ä†
November 12, 2024
Abstract
We provide an overview of an optimal control problem within a stochastic model of tumor growth,
which includes drug application. The model comprises two stochastic differential equations (SDE) repre-
senting the diffusion of nutrient and drug concentrations. To account for various uncertainties, stochastic
terms are incorporated into the deterministic framework, capturing random disturbances. Control vari-
ables, informed by medical principles, are used to regulate drug and nutrient concentrations. In deÔ¨Åning
the optimal control problem, a stochastic cost function can be established, and a Feynman-type path inte-
gral control approach would lead to an optimal drug treatment.

entiÔ¨Åc Ô¨Åelds. Mathematicians in cancer research have approached tumor growth through mathematical mod-
eling from multiple perspectives (Zhao, 2010). Frequently, models assume that tumors expand with radial
symmetry, a concept supported by early in vitro observations showing that initial solid tumor growth tends
to be radially symmetric (Esmaili et al., 2024). Evidence also suggests that low glucose and oxygen levels in

cells (Tao, 2009; Zhao, 2010). Consequently, many tumor models differentiate live cells into proliferative and
quiescent groups. It‚Äôs essential to note that all systems encounter uncertainties due to environmental factors,

The rise of personalized medicine in cancer treatment has transformed therapeutic strategies for patients
with tumors that possess actionable mutations. For some, this shift has signiÔ¨Åcantly extended lifespans, low-
ered toxicity, and enhanced quality of life. Unfortunately, however, this group remains limited; estimates
from 2020 suggest that only around 5% of patients beneÔ¨Åt from targeted therapies (Wang et al., 2024). More-
over, while personalized treatments offer substantial beneÔ¨Åts, they rarely lead to a complete cure, as tumors
often evolve resistance through Darwinian mechanisms (Khan et al., 2024; Vikramdeo et al., 2024). In re-
sponse, a new approach called evolutionary therapy aims to leverage disease evolution dynamics to optimize
drug choices and treatment schedules. Researchers are utilizing both mathematical and experimental models
to explore practical questions of theoretical signiÔ¨Åcance, such as how resistance to one drug inÔ¨Çuences sensi-
tivity to others and whether heterogeneous tumor populations respond differently to treatment based on their
state. Insights from these studies have already informed rational drug sequencing and cycling, beneÔ¨Åting
bacterial infections as well as various cancers. In therapeutic scheduling, adaptive therapy‚Äîrooted in Evolu-
tionary Game Theory (EGT) has shown encouraging results, not only in theory but also in a phase 2 trial for
men with metastatic prostate cancer (Kaznatcheev et al., 2017). Experimentally, the principles of EGT have
been validated in vivo, with ongoing development of in vitro assays and observations of interactive dynamics
using these methods (Wang et al., 2024).
Cancer cells, like other populations of living organisms, consist of individual cells with unique behav-
iors and evolutionary backgrounds. Their interactions and life histories are Ô¨Ålled with stochastic elements,
including genetic variability, transitions in cell fate, differential drug responses, signaling differences, and
micro-scale environmental Ô¨Çuctuations within the tumor. Many of these represent demographic stochasticity,
where random variation can often be averaged out if the population is large enough. This concept underpins
the understanding of tumor heterogeneity by dividing cells into sub-populations (Pramanik, 2024a). Such
2
grouping is effective when mutation-selection dynamics are balanced so that only closely related genotypes
with the same phenotype persist stably (Wang et al., 2024). These clusters, known as quasispecies, form
distributions around a central genotype, with cells in each group displaying similar behavior despite minor
genetic differences and random birth or death events. However, environmental stochasticity, which remains
signiÔ¨Åcant even in large populations as it encompasses random factors that affect entire groups simultane-
ously. In cancer, such Ô¨Çuctuations can result from factors unrelated to treatment, such as small but frequent
changes in the physiology of the host. Moreover, individual cells within subpopulations will still respond
differently to these broad disturbances. Therefore, implementation of environmental stochasticity reÔ¨Çects an
approach focused on modeling the averaged response of subpopulations to these overarching system-wide
inÔ¨Çuences (Pramanik et al., 2024).
When modeling such Ô¨Çuctuations in continuous time, Stochastic Differential Equations (SDEs) often
appear, with Stochastic Control Theory providing a means to optimize their behavior (Pramanik, 2021c;
Pramanik and Polansky, 2021, 2023). This approach gives a mathematical perspective for making sequen-
tial decision making such as determining the appropriate drug dose over time while accounting for random
shocks (Pramanik and Polansky, 2020), like unpredictable shifts in the Ô¨Åtness of competing cancer cell sub-
populations. Any static treatment approach will produce a random evolutionary path for the tumor and a
random cumulative cost (which could represent total drug usage, time to recovery, or a blend of these factors)
(Wang et al., 2024). Dynamic Programming (DP) (Pramanik, 2020; Pramanik and Polansky, 2024b) aims to
create equations for the cumulative cost of the best strategy, allowing that strategy to be expressed in feedback
form, meaning treatment decisions (dose and duration) are updated according to the current state of the tumor
rather than following a predetermined schedule. This approach can be implemented to various cancer models
and treatment types, including strategies aimed at either stabilizing or eradicating the tumor. However, rather
than choosing a strategy that minimizes the average expected cost, one can select the objective function that
maximizes the probability of achieving a speciÔ¨Åc goal (e.g., meeting therapy objectives without surpassing a
set cost limit). These risk-aware, or threshold-aware, policies are designed to adapt the treatment plan based
on the response of the tumor to previous drugs and the cost accumulated up to that point.
McKean Jr (1966) dynamics is highly advantageous in stochastic control under random perturbations,
particularly for systems where interactions are shaped by both individual behaviors and population-level
inÔ¨Çuences (Carmona et al., 2013; Carmona and Delarue, 2015). First, McKean‚ÄìVlasov dynamics enables
3
mean-Ô¨Åeld interactions (Lasry and Lions, 2007), meaning that the behavior of a single cancer cell depends
not only on its own state but also on the state distribution of all other cells (Polansky and Pramanik, 2021;
Pramanik, 2024b). Second, in large systems, modeling each cancer cell individually can be computation-
ally intensive. McKean‚ÄìVlasov dynamics reduces this complexity by focusing on the average effect of the
population, making large-scale stochastic control problems more computationally feasible (Hua et al., 2019).
Third, mean-Ô¨Åeld terms help smooth Ô¨Çuctuations due to random noise, fostering more stable and reliable
treatment policies a major beneÔ¨Åt for systems where high noise sensitivity could otherwise result in erratic
responses (Pramanik, 2022a,b, 2023b,c). Fourth, in multi-host environments where hosts are inÔ¨Çuenced by
their surroundings and each other, McKean‚ÄìVlasov dynamics captures these interdependent behaviors effec-
tively. It allows for both centralized and decentralized strategies to efÔ¨Åciently manage large populations of
interacting hosts (Pramanik and Polansky, 2024c). Fifth, McKean‚ÄìVlasov dynamics is beneÔ¨Åcial for risk-
sensitive or mean-Ô¨Åeld treatment strategies, as it enables the management of not only individual outcomes
but also their distributions. This ability to Ô¨Åne-tune policies to control both mean and variance is particularly
useful when controlling risk (variance) is essential (Pramanik, 2016, 2021b). Lastly, in systems exhibiting
herding effects, McKean‚ÄìVlasov dynamics incorporates self-reinforcing behaviors, where hosts‚Äô actions in-
Ô¨Çuence one another (Pramanik and Polansky, 2024a). This approach is valuable in settings requiring behavior
synchronization or collective dynamics for the desired system outcome.

Concluding remarks:
It is now well understood that cancers adapt and evolve while one person is under therapy, and this under-
standing is gradually being factored into treatment planning. In some instances, adapting treatment can be as
straightforward as switching from one targeted therapy to another. However, instances where tumors consist
of a diverse mix of interacting cell types, such adjustments are often impractical. Therefore, an ecological
approach called adaptive therapy is gaining interest. Until recently, clinical trials and theoretical models of
adaptive therapy have relied on initial assumptions about the interactions within tumors and how they affect
tumor composition over time (Wang et al., 2024). However, a growing number of studies, conducted both in
lab settings and live models, are beginning to establish methods to measure more accurately these interac-
tions. Since these tools advance, the next hurdle will be to understand these dynamics directly in patients and
leverage them to enhance personalized treatment options.
In this paper we suggest an alternative mean-Ô¨Åeld approach driven by a McKean-Vlasov dynamics. Mean
Ô¨Åeld games, while present in various forms for some time, particularly in economics, have their theoretical
roots in the groundbreaking contributions of Lasry and Lions (2006a,b, 2007), as well as Huang et al. (2006).
The central idea is to model the behavior of a large group of optimizing individuals who interact with each
other through mean-Ô¨Åeld dynamics, under certain energetic or economic constraints. This approach simpliÔ¨Åes
the study of consensus by focusing on a control problem for a single representative individual, who interacts
with the environment formed by the collective behavior of others. Essentially, when consensus is achieved,
the system‚Äôs inherent symmetries (Pramanik, 2024c) drive individuals to conform to a type of law of large
numbers, resulting in a propagation of chaos effect as the population size increases (Carmona et al., 2016).
Inspired by the theory of propagation of chaos, it can be anticipated that simpliÔ¨Åed equations might apply as
the number of cancer cells becomes extremely large, making it feasible to work with solutions that are less
complex than seeking Nash equilibria in large-scale stochastic differential games as previously discussed.
This insight forms the basis for deÔ¨Åning the mean Ô¨Åeld game problem. A mean Ô¨Åeld game is fundamentally an
asymptotic framework for the game, where each cell‚Äôs inÔ¨Çuence on the overall empirical measure is minimal.
This suggests that, in the limit, optimization problems may become decoupled and uniform. In other words,
the equilibrium problem at this limit reduces to a standard optimization problem for a single representative
cancer cell, which interacts or competes solely with the environment shaped by the asymptotic behavior
9
of the marginal empirical measures, {Œ∏(s)}s‚àà[0,t], linked to an adaptive treatment {u(s)}s‚àà[0,t] ‚ààU which
is exchangeable. In the absence of common noise, the classical law of large numbers indicates that the
limiting environment would be a deterministic sequence of probability measures, {Œ∏(s)}s‚àà[0,t], representing
the equilibrium distribution of the population. However, when common noise is introduced, its effects do
not cancel out, and thus the limiting environment becomes a stochastic sequence, {Œ∏(s)}s‚àà[0,t], reÔ¨Çecting the
conditional distribution of the population in equilibrium given the common noise. This inclusion of common
noise introduces new possibilities in cancer research by providing a dynamic perspective on the system‚Äôs
behavior.
